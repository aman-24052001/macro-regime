Problem Statement

Why Macro Regime Detection?

The Core Insight

Asset returns are not i.i.d. — they cluster by economic regime. Equities outperform in expansions; bonds in contractions; gold in stagflation. A system that identifies the current regime can rotate capital accordingly, improving risk-adjusted returns without predicting prices.

Traditional static portfolios (60/40) apply fixed allocations regardless of economic cycle. A regime-aware strategy is adaptive by design.

Hypothesis

Macroeconomic state is latent — it cannot be observed directly but leaves a signature across yield curves, credit spreads, labor markets, and inflation. A Hidden Markov Model is a natural fit: hidden states (regimes) drive observable emissions (macro indicators).

Walk-forward validation enforces strict no-look-ahead: the model never sees the future during any OOS prediction.

Interview Preparation

Anticipated Questions & Answers

Non-Technical — Strategy & Motivation
Walk me through this project in 2 minutes.

The central question is: can we improve risk-adjusted returns by detecting economic regimes and rotating asset classes accordingly? I built a full pipeline that pulls 12 macroeconomic series from FRED (yield curves, credit spreads, inflation, unemployment, Fed policy, going back to 1960) and engineers 34 features. A 3-state Gaussian Hidden Markov Model learns to identify latent economic regimes — Expansion, Stagnation, and Contraction — purely from the data.

The model is validated walk-forward: train on 1991–2005, predict 2006–2026, one month at a time, never looking ahead. Regime probabilities drive a probability-weighted ETF allocation across SPY, TLT, GLD, LQD, HYG, and BIL.

Result: 8.45% CAGR, Sharpe 0.937, max drawdown -21% — significantly better risk-adjusted than the SPY buy-and-hold (Sharpe 0.76, max DD -51%). The strategy delivered +3.4% during the GFC when SPY lost -46%.

Why use a HMM rather than a simpler rules-based regime model?

A rules-based approach would require me to specify the thresholds: "inverted yield curve for 3 months = contraction." That embeds my prior assumptions directly. The HMM learns the regime boundaries from the data — the emission means and transition probabilities are estimated, not assumed.

Additionally, the HMM provides soft assignments — probability P(regime|data) rather than a binary flag. This allows smooth weight transitions and avoids whipsawing. It also provides uncertainty quantification: if P(expansion)=0.55 and P(stagnation)=0.40, the portfolio blends accordingly rather than going all-in on expansion.

What is the biggest weakness of this strategy?

The "Contraction" regime label is misleading. The HMM identifies it as a late-cycle tightening state — inverted yield curve, hot labor market, high Fed Funds — which is a precursor to recession, not an actual recession. The portfolio allocates TLT=40% in this state, which was designed for falling rates during an actual downturn.

In 2022, when the model correctly flagged late-cycle conditions, the Fed was hiking rates aggressively and TLT fell 30%+. The strategy lost -16.5% versus SPY's -18.2% — marginally better, but Equal Weight did better at -13.3%. This is the most important thing I'd fix: either add a rate-direction signal (rising rates → reduce duration), or re-calibrate the Contraction portfolio (TLT 40% → 20%, BIL 20% → 40%).

Why K=3 states? How did you choose it?

Two complementary signals:

  • BIC marginal improvement elbow: BIC scores (extended 1960 data, 229 months × 7 features) — K2: 4144.6, K3: 3786.5, K4: 3573.6, K5: 3528.7. Gains: K2→K3: 358.1 pts, K3→K4: 212.9 pts, K4→K5: 44.9 pts. The sharp drop at K4→K5 (79% smaller) marks the statistical elbow. Economic prior of K=3 retained — three phases (expansion, stagnation, contraction) have clear precedent in NBER cycles and portfolio theory.
  • Economic prior: K=3 maps cleanly to Expansion / Stagnation / Contraction — three states that span the full business cycle and correspond directly to the regime target portfolios in config.

BIC is reported honestly (BIC selects K=5) and the override is logged explicitly — preserving transparency for this kind of interview review.

Technical — Model & Implementation
How do you prevent look-ahead bias?
  • Walk-forward expanding window: At each OOS date T, the model is retrained on all data with index < T. It never sees any future observations.
  • One-period weight lag: Weights computed from regime probabilities at T are applied to returns at T+1. The line in code: weights_lagged = target_weights.shift(1).
  • No k-fold: Cross-validation on time series leaks future into past. Walk-forward is the only valid approach.
  • Feature engineering: All features use only past data (rolling windows, differences, lags). No future-referencing transformations.
Why did BIC give degenerate results initially, and how did you fix it?

The first run passed all 39 engineered features to BIC. With K=5 and 39 features using full covariance, the parameter count is:

K×(K-1) + (K-1) + K×39 + K×39×40/2 = ~4119 params with only ~3120 data points — parameter-to-data ratio > 1.0. The covariance matrices were degenerate and BIC was meaningless.

Fix: run BIC only on the 7-feature HMM subset. With K=3 and 7 features, the parameter count is 113 vs 229×7=1603 data scalars — ratio of 0.07, well-conditioned. This is also why the same 7 features are used throughout the walk-forward training.

What caused the walk-forward to produce zero predictions initially?

The code called trainer.walk_forward(features.dropna(), ...). The global dropna() applied across all 39 columns. Two binary flag columns (yc_inverted, yc_10y3m_inverted) had 142 and 170 NaN values respectively in the early period, because the T10Y3M FRED series starts later.

After dropna(), only ~80 rows survived — nearly all pre-1995. The OOS window (2006+) had only ~14 rows, and the 180-month training threshold was never met. Fix: pass the full DataFrame and let the walk-forward function handle NaN internally per training window, per feature subset.

Why are some hmmlearn "not converging" warnings expected and safe to ignore?

The EM algorithm has a convergence tolerance tol. The warning fires when the log-likelihood improvement in the final iteration is smaller than zero — meaning the algorithm took a tiny step backwards from numerical noise. The key diagnostic is the delta magnitude:

  • Deltas like -6e-05 or -2e-07 are floating-point noise at convergence — safe to ignore.
  • A delta like -101.5 (observed in one K=5 restart) indicates a genuinely bad EM trajectory — but with 50 random restarts, we select the best log-likelihood, so poor restarts are discarded.

The 50-restart strategy is the safeguard: as long as some restarts converge to a good solution, the BIC and walk-forward results are reliable.

Walk me through a specific bug you found and fixed.

The rebalancer date index bug — a subtle but serious one.

The rebalancer loops over all dates in asset_returns.index (which starts from 1993) and skips pre-OOS dates via continue. After collecting 241 results, it built the DataFrame with: pd.DataFrame(results, index=asset_returns.index[:241]). This took the first 241 dates of all returns — approximately 1993 to 2013 — even though the results were computed for 2006 to 2026.

The consequence: the crisis analysis found the strategy for Dot-com (2000–2002) and GFC (2007–2009) but it was actually showing the 2006-2007 and 2009-2011 returns with wrong labels. Taper Tantrum, COVID, and 2022 rows were entirely missing.

Fix: track a dates_used list alongside results, appending each date per iteration. Use pd.DataFrame(results, index=dates_used). One line change, but caught only by examining the crisis analysis output.

How do you validate that the regime labels are economically meaningful?

Auto-labelling is data-driven: states are ranked by their mean emission on yield_curve_10y2y. Highest slope → Expansion, lowest → Contraction. This avoids label-switching across walk-forward windows.

Validation:

  • Emission means: Decode the means for all 7 features per state. Contraction should show inverted curve + high rates + tight spreads (or elevated FSI). This checks out.
  • Historical overlay: The regime timeline should assign Contraction to 2007–08, 2018–19, 2022; Expansion to 2010–12. This broadly matches.
  • NBER comparison: Compute the lag between model regime entry and NBER official recession dates. Target: ≤3 months.
  • Sanity check: GFC performance (+3.4%) confirms the model held defensive assets during the actual drawdown.
If you had more time, what would you improve first?
  1. Fix the Contraction portfolio: Reduce TLT from 40% to 15–20%, raise BIL to 35–40%. This directly addresses the 2022 weakness without changing the model.
  2. Add a rate-direction signal: If Fed Funds momentum is positive (rates rising), reduce duration in the Contraction portfolio. This separates "late-cycle tightening" from "rate-cut recession."
  3. ALFRED vintage data: Use ALFRED real-time vintages for GDP and CPI to eliminate revision bias. The current implementation uses final revised data — in real-time, GDP Q1 isn't available until July.
  4. GARCH volatility overlay: Use GARCH(1,1) rolling vol forecasts to scale down equity exposure when realized vol is elevated, independent of regime.

Evaluation Framework

Metrics We Use & What They Mean

Primary — Risk-Adjusted Return

Sharpe Ratio  0.937  SPY: 0.760

(CAGR − Rf) ÷ Annualised Volatility

The standard portfolio metric — return per unit of total volatility. Our 0.937 vs SPY's 0.760 = +23% more return per unit of risk. This is the first number any recruiter or PM will ask about.

Limitation: penalises upside vol equally to downside. For a drawdown-protection strategy, Sortino is more honest.

Downside Protection

Sortino Ratio  1.357  SPY: 1.050

(CAGR − Rf) ÷ Downside Deviation (returns < 0)

Return per unit of bad volatility only. Our 1.357 vs 1.050 = +29% better. Preferred over Sharpe for regime strategies where upside volatility is intentionally asymmetric — we want to cut the left tail, not the right.

A Sortino > 1.0 means we generate more than 1% of return per 1% of downside risk — the strategy is paying for itself.

Tail Risk — Institutional Mandate Metric

Calmar Ratio  0.402  SPY: 0.208

CAGR ÷ |Max Drawdown|

Annual return earned per unit of worst-case loss. This is what trustees and CIOs care about — not average vol, but the maximum they can lose. Our 0.402 vs SPY's 0.208 = +93% better. For every 1% of peak-to-trough loss, we generate 0.402% of annual return vs 0.208% for SPY.

A Calmar > 0.5 is generally considered strong; we're at 0.402 — solid for a macro rotation strategy with real TC friction.

Capital Preservation — Compounding Math

Max Drawdown  −21.0%  SPY: −50.8%

(Trough − Peak) ÷ Peak  ·  worst over full OOS period

A −50.8% drawdown requires a +103% gain just to get back to even. A −21.0% drawdown needs only +27%. The compounding math is brutal: avoiding the GFC crash means never needing the subsequent multi-year bull run just to break even.

Max DD duration: 31 months for us vs 74 months for SPY — we recovered 2.4× faster from peak to new high.

⚠ The Honest Limitation — 2022 Rate Shock

The strategy lost −16.5% in 2022 — only marginally better than SPY's −18.2%, and worse than Equal Weight's −13.3%. The root cause is a portfolio design assumption embedded in the Contraction regime: TLT = 40%, which assumes falling rates calibrated for an actual recession (Fed cutting). In 2022, the Fed hiked 525bps in 12 months and TLT fell 30%+. The HMM correctly identified late-cycle conditions, but the Contraction portfolio was built for the wrong flavour of contraction.

Proposed fix (not implemented, by design): Add a rate-direction signal. If Fed Funds momentum is positive (rates rising), re-route Contraction allocation: TLT 40% → 15%, BIL 20% → 40%, GLD 25% → 25% (hold). This separates late-cycle tightening (still hiking — rates rising) from actual recession (Fed cutting — duration rewarded). The 2022 scenario is the first; the 2008 GFC is the second. The current model conflates both into a single Contraction state.

Why not fixed: This would require either a 4th HMM state (tightening vs. recession contraction) or a conditional overlay signal.
